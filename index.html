Do A Barrel Roll??

Summary

“Do A Barrel Roll??” is a “Getting over it” type of platformer developed via Unity Engine. It introduces a unique control method of character and challenging level design with a punishing penalty when making a mistake. This project was developed solo in 9 months with a minimum of downloaded assets. All game scripts, tiles, UI elements, main menu background, barrel sprite and pirate ship sprite are created by myself.

Programming



During development, I found that the emoji movement requires some complicated physics calculations. At first, I defined realistic physics from scratch. However, the movement was boring. So, I make the emoji movement controlled by two different methods where the effectiveness of the methods change depending on the barrel's angular velocity. As a result, the movement of emoji becomes ridiculous and funny but at the same time, still realistic enough.

On the other hand, there is a bug where the character sometimes gets stuck inside the tilemap when high in velocity. Sometimes, Unity's physics engine itself is enough to save the situation. However, it is far from consistent. Therefore, I try to move the character to the opposite direction of its velocity to free it when it gets stuck in more than one frame. As the engine may fix it itself. As a result, the bug never showed again. Providing a smooth gameplay experience to the player.

Gameplay Design



I tend to design the gameplay to a very detailed level. But the downside is, it requires a large number of parameters. As a result, the goal of design becomes very vague. To address this, I created a few situations that need to be satisfied. And the goal would be finding a parameter set that satisfies all the situations. For example, in this game, I want the player to climb a 3 by 1 slope with the requirement of drawing 1-2 circles per second so that it is very possible but requires a little bit of effort. At the same time, after falling from a 1 by 3 slope I want the player to be able to stop in 3 - 4 flat tiles length so that player has to really try and always worry of not stopping in time. At the end, after many trials of play testing, I found the parameter set to satisfy both situations and fit the goal of my gameplay design.

Level Design



Regarding this type of game, I always think that the player can't do anything after a mistake is unfair. The different outcome only depends on the position of the mistake being made. Therefore, I want to design the level so that after making a mistake. Though some progress will be lost but the amount of progress loss depends on players actions. However, it is very difficult to design such levels. So I first sketch the platforming idea part of the section. Then link the section to the previous section and consider the potential failure and their outcome. Next, add appropriate potential save to the previous section or the current section. Lastly, repeat between play testing the level and fine tuning the level until I am satisfied with the result. At the end, I provide a challenging and sometimes frustrating but always fair experience to the players.

Elementalist

Summary

“Elementalist” is a mouse only arcade game developed with Unity Engine during ScoreSpace Jam#26. In this game, players play as a mage who collects elemental spirits to perform spells to defeat enemies. Players would click on the spirits that flew around the screen to collect them. Then drag the collected spirits to the enemy to cast spells. According to the number and types of spirits the player has collected, different unique spells can be casted.

Programming



During the development. Determining which spells are cast by the collected spirit was a challenge. The most straightforward way would be checking every spell with every collected spirit for each element. However, this is terrible in time efficiency with O(n^2).Therefore, I came up with two other solutions.

The first one is having an array of 4 integers. As the number of collected spirits for each element. Then loop through the spell list and check for spell cost. This achieves a time efficiency O(n) implementation with an array of 4 integers added into the memory.

The second solution is having a 4 dimension array as a spell list. Each slot of the array would be null or a spell item. Then again, having an array of 4 integers to count the collected spirits. So that, when a player casts the spell, I just need to find the item in the spell list with a time efficiency of O(1). However, the highest cost spell cost 4 spirits of each element. Therefore, a minimum of 256 slots will be allocated in the spell list. Which takes some memory.

In the end, I chose the first solution. As a game for game jam, I did not prepare a lot of spells i.e. 14 spells. Therefore, the majority in the spell list from the second solution would be empty. Which is not worth doing. In addition, for the same reason, in the worst case, n would only be 14 in the first solution. Although the second solution is convenient to show the spell player can be cast with current collected spirits. However, as a game plan for a WebGL build, I do not want to take too much memory. Therefore, the first solution is more suitable for this situation.

Gameplay Design



During the design process of this game, there are a few aspects that I try to achieve. Those are rewarding resources management, minimum downtime boredom and providing game progression.

To reward players that manage the spirits on screen well, there is an elemental weakness system. The enemy has an elemental type. As well as the 4 spells that cost 2 of the same element. Which deals 150%, 100%, 50% and 0% damage respectively regarding enemy elemental type. In this case, the most optimal strategy would be to always focus on enemy weaknesses. However, the spirit on the screen may not always be ideal. Therefore, there are also two spells that cost 3 spirit which alway deal 100% damage. Furthermore, spells with 3 or 4 different types of spirit would spawn 3 or in the latter case, 5 onto the screen. As a result, players have more control over the battle and minimize the effect of luck to achieve rewarding game play.

Secondly, if there is no downtime for the player, it may be too chaotic and stressful for the player. However, if there is too much downtime without anything to do, it also affects the fluency of the game play. To address that, enabling the player to swap around the spirit is just perfect for the downtime. During the down time, the enemy is still waiting to be spawned. At that time, no stress would be added onto the player. But the player can manage around the spirit, increasing their advantage for the next fight. Therefore, providing a resting time without causing boredom of the player.

Lastly, I would like to provide a sense of progression to the player, even if this is a small arcade game. As a result, spells are designed to be unlocked in the library after the player has casted the spell once. Enable players to improve by learning more knowledge of the spells and providing a discovery kind of game play experience.
